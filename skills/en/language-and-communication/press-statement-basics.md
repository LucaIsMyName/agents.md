# press-statement-basics.md

## Purpose

This document explains **how to write clear, professional press statements**.

The goal: communicate information that is **accurate, credible, and easy to understand** — especially for journalists and the public.

---

## Core Mindset

A press statement is:

* **informational, not promotional**
* **clear, not clever**
* **structured, not free-form**

👉 You are not writing marketing copy — you are communicating facts.

---

## 1. Lead With the Facts

### Rule

The first paragraph should answer:

* Who?
* What?
* When?
* Where?
* Why?

### ❌ Bad

```text
We are excited to announce something amazing.
```

### ✅ Good

```text
Acme Inc. announced the launch of a new AI platform on Monday in London.
```

👉 The reader should understand the core message immediately.

---

## 2. Write a Strong Headline

### Rule

The headline should be:

* clear
* specific
* factual

### ❌ Bad

```text
Revolutionizing the Future of Innovation
```

### ✅ Good

```text
Acme Inc. Launches New AI Platform for Data Analysis
```

👉 Avoid vague or exaggerated language.

---

## 3. Keep It Concise

### Rule

Use short sentences and paragraphs.

### ❌ Bad

```text
The company, which has been operating successfully for many years, is now proud to introduce a groundbreaking new solution that...
```

### ✅ Good

```text
The company introduced a new solution. It improves data processing speed.
```

👉 One idea per sentence.

---

## 4. Stay Neutral and Professional

### Rule

Avoid hype, exaggeration, or emotional language.

### ❌ Bad

```text
An incredible, game-changing product!
```

### ✅ Good

```text
A new product with improved performance.
```

👉 Let facts speak for themselves.

---

## 5. Structure Matters

### Typical Structure

1. Headline
2. Lead paragraph (W-questions)
3. Supporting details
4. Quote (optional)
5. Closing / additional info
6. Contact information

👉 Readers expect this structure.

---

## 6. Add Useful Details (But Only Relevant Ones)

### Include:

* context
* impact
* key features
* background

### Avoid:

* unnecessary history
* internal jargon
* irrelevant details

👉 Every sentence should add value.

---

## 7. Use Quotes Strategically

### Purpose

* add human voice
* provide interpretation or emphasis

### Example

```text
"This launch represents a major step forward," said the CEO.
```

👉 Quotes should add meaning, not repeat facts.

---

## 8. Avoid Jargon and Buzzwords

### ❌ Bad

```text
A scalable, cutting-edge, next-generation solution
```

### ✅ Good

```text
A system that can handle growing amounts of data
```

👉 Write for a general audience, not insiders.

---

## 9. Be Precise

### Rule

Use concrete, verifiable statements.

### ❌ Bad

```text
Soon, the product will change everything.
```

### ✅ Good

```text
The product will be available starting June 2026.
```

👉 Specifics build credibility.

---

## 10. Keep It Short

### Rule

A press statement should be:

* typically 150–400 words
* focused on one main message

👉 If it’s too long, it won’t be read.

---

## 11. End With Clear Next Steps

### Include:

* availability (when/where)
* contact info
* links (if applicable)

👉 Make it easy to follow up.

---

## 12. Common Footguns

### 🚨 Too much marketing tone

→ Sounds like an ad → loses credibility

### 🚨 Burying the main message

→ Put key info first

### 🚨 Long, complex sentences

→ Hard to read → easy to misunderstand

### 🚨 No clear structure

→ Confuses the reader

### 🚨 Vague statements

→ Reduces trust

---

## Final Thought

Good press statements feel:

* clear
* factual
* easy to scan

Bad ones feel:

* exaggerated
* confusing
* bloated

👉 If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it.
